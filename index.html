Most people developing distributed evolutionary algorithms think that the computing world is a little bit like that. Except the cat, that is. Regular, identical, static, always the same. Nothing changes and you can do stuff like parallel evolutionary algorithm of GPGPU or cluster computing or grid. Pretty straighforward, synchronous, predictable.
Of course, it's bit more like this. Nodes have different sizes, they are distributed unevenly and have different number of connections, and are as fragile as this spiderweb. It's also as numerous as the junctions or as the droplets in it, much more massive. And maybe you could take advantage of it.
This is a tagcloud of the same old. Old tools, old systems, proprietary software.
This is the environment you see today on the edge of the big data/cloud revolution (or maybe just evolution). New languages, new paradigms, new methodologies for developing software. Can we do something about it?
First, we have to realize what's the nature of this new world. It's no longer fine grain or coarse grain or whatever. There are all kinds of grains, but in particular we have three different types of technologies that we might want to address: peer to peer, cloud computing and cycle scavenging/volunteer computing.
The clouds exemplify very well what goes on in this new computing environment. First, because a part of it is literally called cloud. Second, because they are out there and there are lots of them, but they are predictable only in the short term and also ephemeral, here this moment, gone the next.
That's another characteristic of this new environment: it's available on tap. You pay cloud computing resources, CPU, storage, other more advanced applications like Map/Reduce, by the hour or minute. This means two things: you can have as much as you want, and second, this comes at a cost. Your algorithms will have to be scale and cost conscious, because you might be eventually wasting your grant just on computing resources.
Docker is one of the emerging technologies that are being used for application development and deployment: it allows safe containing of all the context needed for an application. And then there are microservices: independently deployable applications which, together, form an application that is perceived as such by the user.
In many cases, asynchrony is also the norm. First, because you can't expect virtual machines to start at exactly the same time or behave in exactly the same way, and they're spending while they are running. Second, because in the case of volunteer systems they might simply not be available. Even distributed computing can't work in lockstep, and there must be some way of permanently storing the algorithm state (or part of it) so that it can resume when resources are available.
The good thing is that you can gather a incredible amount of resources for free or a low price. A full month of a server running in Azure or Amazon can be a few dollars, a little bit more than a hosting. You can do a lot of things with the free tier of Platform as a Service or Infrastructure as a service. And, finally, if you go for volunteer computing the amount of resources can be unlimited in terms of computing nodes, although very limited in terms of time
So what we're looking at is a big pile of self-star properties: self-assembly, self-healing, self-adaptation... the most self-* the algorithm is, the better.
A way of solving the stuff is using autnomous agents; this basically means, from the point of view of the algorithm, to avoid a central authority: every thread or process has to decide all its lifecycle: when to die, when to give birth, mix, everything.
Then you have to deal with peer to peer, dynamic networks, where there is no fixed topology and where every individual, autonomously, decides to interchange stuff with another, as in this string and nails artifact where strings might vary... and nails too.
You can obtain computing power aplenty this way, but it comes at a cost: you have to change the methodology for developing software. Team/test/agile based using infrastructe-as-code/build/deployment tools. It's a small cost, since it makes your science, eventually, better and more reprodutible.

For instance, you might have to use something like this to declare the infraestructure needed by a program. This is a very simple package.json for deploying a volunteer-based evolutionary algorithm, Nodio, in a PaaS such as OpenShift or Heroku (both free). It includes instructions for testing and then all dependencies. There are other interesting trends nowadays: using Docker containers to package applications along with all the context.
Volunteer computing is no longer a simple entertainment. It graduated wit SETI@home and there are several patents that cover its use in browsers. And there are browsers everywhere. And, what is more importante, you can achieve a certain amount of computation without paying any money, although it's not going to be free.
This is what you can obtain in an experiment a Sunday morning (and afternoon). Several hundreds of different volunteers, and even more so, some of then lending hundreds of thousands of generations to the experiment.
Cloud is the way to go and the best way to exemplify the current state of computation. Which we will have to embrace, eventually, if we want to suceed implementing metaheuristics in the large.
